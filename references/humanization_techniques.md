# Humanization Techniques

All rhetorical, structural, and probabilistic techniques for generating
authentic Institutional thesis voice.

---

## 2.0 The 0% Formula (CRITICAL — APPLY FIRST)

Confirmed from two independent 0%-scoring thesis samples (Rice Thermography,
VOICE-B). These are not stylistic suggestions. They are the structural
backbone of undetectable thesis writing. Apply all rules in this section
before any other technique.

### 2.0.1 The 5-Connector Rotation (MANDATORY)

Every paragraph must cycle through these sentence-starter connectors in
IRREGULAR order. Never use the same connector twice in a row. Never open
three consecutive paragraphs with the same connector.

| Connector | Function | Example |
|---|---|---|
| `Also,` | Add supporting point | "Also, the Researcher finds that aging interferes..." |
| `Because of that,` | State logical result | "Because of that, it is hard for this method to catch..." |
| `In this case,` | Zoom into specifics | "In this case, the system uses a conveyor belt..." |
| `Since [clause],` | Causal frame opener | "Since these non-viable seeds have broken internal structures..." |
| `Basically,` | Plain summary or explanation | "Basically, the Researcher sees that the way they absorb..." |
| `So,` | Wrap up or conclude | "So, the Researcher observed that these techniques are just too slow..." |
| `Moreover,` | Formal addition (Ch1 only) | "Moreover, this is a manual process..." |
| `Additionally,` | RRL/RRS addition | "Additionally, [Author et al. (Year)] used deep learning..." |
| `While [clause],` | Concessive contrast | "While this is quick, the Researcher found that it is destructive." |

**Rotation rule**: Within a single paragraph, at least 2 different connectors
must appear. A paragraph of 4+ sentences must use at least 3 different
connectors.

### 2.0.2 The "which means" Glue (MANDATORY)

This is the single most confirmed pattern across all 0% samples. Nearly
every sentence must contain "which means [consequence]" embedded mid-sentence
or at the end.

**Formula**: [Fact or finding], which means [consequence or implication].

Examples from 0% samples:
- "...which means the eggs can still hatch after the sex is identified early on"
- "...which means that image-based classification is a feasible method"
- "...which means they can no longer function normally"
- "...which means they can be distinguished from dead or low-vigour seeds"

**Frequency**: At least one "which means" per paragraph. Two is acceptable.
Three or more per paragraph is too many.

DO NOT replace with: "therefore," "thus," "hence," or "consequently."
Those are AI patterns. Use "which means" only.

### 2.0.3 The Reason Chain Structure (MANDATORY)

AI writing separates cause and effect into clean independent sentences.
Human writing chains them. Use this two-sentence pattern as the backbone
of every explanation paragraph:

```
[Cause] because [reason], which means [consequence].
Because of that, [next action or implication].
```

Example from 0% sample:
"Because these membranes fail, the seeds lose their selective permeability
and start leaking electrolytes, which means they can no longer function.
Because of that, the Researcher must account for this in data collection."

### 2.0.4 Casual Register Injection (MANDATORY)

One informal or casual word or phrase must appear inside every academic
sentence approximately once per paragraph. This is NOT an error. It is
a deliberate register drop that breaks the uniform formal tone of AI writing.

Confirmed casual words from 0% samples:
- "just" ("just too slow", "just beyond the visible light spectrum")
- "a lot of" ("a lot of bottlenecks", "a lot of time")
- "work fine" ("these methods work fine but...")
- "stuck on" ("still stuck on manual")
- "very" as plain intensifier ("very useful", "very subtle", "very different")
- "really" ("really depends on", "can't really keep up")
- "actually" ("these are actually very effective")
- "quite" ("quite effective at classifying")

Rule: ONE casual word per paragraph. Never cluster two in the same sentence.

### 2.0.5 Short Declarative Anchor Sentence (MANDATORY)

Every paragraph must contain at least one short, blunt, plain-language
declarative sentence. This resets the rhythm and signals human paragraph
pacing.

Examples from 0% samples:
- "These old ways create a lot of bottlenecks in modern agriculture."
- "Manual inspection has similar issues."
- "The data said otherwise."
- "These methods work fine but they also destroy the seeds."

Target length: 8-14 words. Placement: anywhere in paragraph except last
sentence. Frequency: once per paragraph.

### 2.0.6 The RRL Paragraph Formula (MANDATORY for Ch2)

Every RRL paragraph follows this exact sequence. No exceptions.

```
STEP 1 — Citation opener:
[Author et al. (Year)] + past tense verb + what they did.

STEP 2 — Zoom in:
"In this case, [what the study specifically focused on]
because [reason]."

STEP 3 — Finding with consequence:
"The researchers/authors found that [result], which means
[implication]."

STEP 4 — Relevance or limitation:
"Because of that, [why this matters to the current study]"
OR
"Also, [limitation or contrast], which means [gap]."
```

Paragraph length: 3-4 sentences MAXIMUM per literature source. Never
write a 5-sentence RRL paragraph. If more information is needed, start
a new paragraph for the same source.

## 2.2 Sentence Rhythm — Rhetorical Function Mapping (MANDATORY)

Before writing each paragraph, identify its dominant rhetorical function.

| Rhetorical Function | Natural Length Range | Use When |
|---|---|---|
| **Emphasis / Claim** | 8-12 words | Introducing core argument, naming gap, stating key finding |
| **Definition** | 18-30 words | Defining terms, citing regulatory standards |
| **Evidence** | 15-25 words | Reporting statistics, study findings, observed data |
| **Contrast / Pivot** | 8-15 words | Transitioning between conflicting claims |
| **Synthesis / Implication** | 12-20 words | Connecting findings to recommendations |

Adjacent paragraphs must have different dominant functions.

### Sharp Punch Sentence Rule

Inject one very short sentence of roughly 4-6 words approximately once every 5
paragraphs. Randomize placement. Do not place at the same position within a
paragraph.
Examples: "The data said otherwise." / "Nothing was there." / "The damage was
invisible."

## 2.3 Embodied Specificity Mandate (CRITICAL)

Every time a technical or abstract claim is made about cost, harm, scale, or
benefit, ask: *What does this look like for one specific household in [the municipality]?*

Minimum frequency: At least one embodied specificity per 250 words in
Introduction and Results.

Must involve one of:
- A specific object (refrigerator, electric water pump)
- A specific person/role (barangay health worker, farmer, household owner)
- A specific price or time duration (2,000 pesos, one month's salary, 30 minutes)
- A specific place name ([the cooperative], [the region], [the municipality], [the province])

---

## 2.4 Problem Genealogy Architecture (INTRODUCTION)

Trace the problem through overlapping causal lenses:
1. Everyday problem — what people experience and call it
2. Terminological work — precision-seeking definition paragraph
3. Infrastructure/systemic cause — aging systems, organizational limitations
4. Demand/economic cause — growth exceeding investment
5. Environmental/geographic cause — typhoons, location, seasonality
6. Warning/detection gap — the experiential absence of alerts
7. Economic consequence (embodied) — what families actually lose
8. Existing solution limitations — what the market offers and fails at
9. Study response — "That gap is what this study targets/addresses"

## 2.5 Paragraph Structure — Functional Architecture

| Pattern | Architecture | Best For |
|---|---|---|
| **Claim-Evidence-Interpretation** | State claim, cite evidence, explain what it means | Evidence-dominant paragraphs |
| **Observation-Explanation** | Describe what you saw, explain why it matters | Results paragraphs |
| **Fact-Meaning** | Drop a key fact, interpret its significance | Statistics, regulatory data |
| **Contrast-Pivot** | Describe what EXISTS, pivot to what is MISSING | Gap statements |
| **Fragment-Expand** | Short punchy statement, expand with context | Problem statements |
| **Stream-of-thought** | One idea leads to next without rigid framing | Synthesis, background |

Same structure may not appear more than twice consecutively.

## 2.7 Spontaneous Thought Patterns

### Pattern 1 — Mid-paragraph thought interruption
A sentence beginning with "Which," "And," or "But" as if the writer just
realized something.
Frequency: 1x per 3-4 paragraphs in Introduction and Results. Rare elsewhere.

### Pattern 2 — Parenthetical aside (embedded naturally, no parentheses)
A clause inserted mid-sentence as a clarifying thought.
Example: "no normal user bothers to measure" / "which most households will not
notice until it is too late"
Frequency: 1x per 2-3 paragraphs.

### Pattern 3 — Slightly resigned or observational tone
Example: "The researchers looked for published technical data from inside [the cooperative]'s
service territory and found nothing."
Frequency: 1x per section, concentrated in Introduction and Results.

### Pattern 4 — Active search-and-find narration
Example: "What the researchers found when they looked for published technical data
was nothing."
Frequency: 1x per section where a gap is being established.

## 2.13 Soul by Named Section

**Introduction** — Show why the researchers care. Connect to real Filipino
experiences. Problem genealogy at full expression. Spontaneous thought patterns
at full frequency.

**Statement of the Problem** — Soul is in what the researchers chose to call out.
End: "That gap is what this study targets/addresses."

**Objectives** — Soul lives only in the specificity of the objectives. Precise
verbs only.

**Scope and Delimitation** — Sound like a student who knows exactly what they
built and what they did not. Honest, not defensive.

**Significance** — Name the barangay health worker, the [the cooperative] technician, the
household owner. Specificity is soul here.

**RRL** — Personality lives in the gap statement and synthesis. "While Author X
achieved Y, what remained unaddressed was..."

**RRS** — Honest about what prior studies got right and what they missed.

**Synthesis and Gap** — Soul peaks here. "What the researchers found when they
reviewed the literature was a consistent absence of..." Last sentence names the
gap this study bridges — not "will bridge."

**Conceptual Framework** — Soul is in the precision of the IPO model labels.

**Definition of Terms** — Operational definitions only. Each term defined as used
in this study.

**Research Design** — Soul only in rationale sentences: "The researchers chose
this approach because..."

**Data Gathering Procedure** — RAD format. Soul is in why each phase was
structured the way it was.

**Instrumentation** — Name everything. "ESP32" not "a microcontroller." Soul is
in knowing exactly what was used and why.

**Statistical Tools** — Each tool justified: "The researchers used [tool] to
[purpose]."

**Results and Discussion** — Full interpretive commentary. Honest about what the
numbers mean. Maximum soul in interpretation.

**Summary of Findings** — Do not oversell. If something underperformed, say so
plainly.

**Conclusions** — Each conclusion tied to a specific finding. No vague
generalization.

**Recommendations** — Sound like advice to a real classmate. Honest about
limitations.

## 2.14 Triple-Fire Protocol (Silent Pre-Output Filter)

This protocol runs silently on every sentence before it is output. It is not
a triggered repair mode. It is a mandatory generation-time filter. No sentence
leaves the draft pass without clearing all three checks.

### The Three Passes

| Pass | What It Checks | Action If Flagged |
|---|---|---|
| Pass 1: Voice Check | Is the subject framing passive-report style? ("This study investigates", "It was found that", "The model establishes") | Reframe subject. Shift agency to researchers or to the object of study. Make the verb active and forward-moving. |
| Pass 2: Vocab Check | Does the sentence contain known AI-signal words? | Replace before output. No exceptions. |
| Pass 3: Structure Check | Is this a long compound sentence with an embedded parenthetical definition, or two clauses joined by "and" with no restructuring? | Break at the junction. Relocate the embedded definition as a standalone appositive or a follow-up clause. Reanchor the second clause with its own subject. |

All three passes fire on every sentence. A sentence that clears only one or
two passes is still detectable. The filter is not optional and not announced.

### Known AI-Signal Word List (Pass 2 Targets)

See **Section 2.1 Master Vocabulary Replacement List** — that is the single
canonical list. Do not maintain a separate copy here. Apply the full table
from 2.1 during Pass 2.

### Secondary Use: Targeted Sentence Repair

If the student submits an externally flagged sentence ("this got detected",
"reduce this", "fix this sentence"), apply the same three passes in sequence
as a repair operation, then reassemble the paragraph for rhythm check.

### Example ([external tutorial, 2024] — 69% to 0%)

**Original sentence (69% AI detected):**

This study investigates consumers' intentions to use over-the-top (OTT) video
streaming platforms based on the extended UTAUT-2 (unified theory of acceptance
and use of technology-2) model and establishes a comprehensive framework for
evaluating OTT video streaming services.

**Pass 1 — Voice Check:**
"This study investigates" is passive-report voice. Reframe: "The current study
aims to examine." Subject shifts. Verb becomes forward-moving and purposive.

**Pass 2 — Vocab Check:**
- "investigates" flagged → "examine"
- "intentions to use" flagged → "usage intention"
- "establishes" flagged → "develop"
- "comprehensive" flagged → "holistic"
- "framework for evaluating" flagged → "evaluation model"

**Pass 3 — Structure Check:**
One compound sentence, two clauses joined by "and", acronym definition embedded
mid-clause as parenthetical. Action: break at the "and" junction. Spell out
UTAUT-2 fully and restructure it as an appositive in the second clause.
Reanchor second clause with its own subject verb ("apply the extended...").

**Output sentence (0% AI detected):**

The current study aims to examine consumers' over-the-top (OTT) video streaming
platform usage intention and apply the extended unified theory of acceptance and
use of technology version 2 (UTAUT-2) to develop a holistic evaluation model
for video streaming services.

### What the example proves

- Spelling out an acronym fully inside a restructured clause lowers detection
  more than keeping the parenthetical shorthand
- Collapsing "intentions to use" into the noun compound "usage intention" removes
  one AI phrasing signal while shortening the sentence
- Breaking the compound at the "and" junction and giving the second clause its
  own verb ("apply") breaks the AI clause-stacking signature
- No synonym was chosen randomly. Each replacement targets a specific AI-signal
  word with a functional equivalent that has lower probability in AI corpora

## Cross-Paragraph Irregularity Rules

1. No two consecutive paragraphs may share the same dominant Rhetorical Function
2. No two consecutive paragraphs may share the same Paragraph Structure
3. Connector density must differ between consecutive paragraphs
4. Vocabulary register may repeat, but not more than twice in a row
5. Paragraph openings must vary — no three consecutive paragraphs may begin with
   the same grammatical pattern

## 2.15 Author Turnitin Resistance Patterns (CONFIRMED 0% SOURCE)

Extracted from the author PhD thesis, independently verified at 0%
on Turnitin. These are structural properties of the writing, not surface
style. Apply them as a layer on top of the existing humanization system.

### 2.15.1 Lexical Repetition Tolerance (CRITICAL)

Do NOT paraphrase key thesis terms to avoid repetition. Repeat them exactly.

Detectors flag paraphrase diversity as an AI signal. Human writers repeat
their core construct because they are too focused on the idea to swap
synonyms. If the key term is "conservative agriculture practices," write
"conservative agriculture practices" every time. Do not substitute
"sustainable farming methods," "eco-friendly agricultural approaches," or
any other synonym.

Rule: The main research construct appears in identical form across all
paragraphs. Zero synonym substitution for that term.

### 2.15.2 Clunky Passive Construction

Use non-standard passive forms that no LLM would generate naturally.

Instead of clean passive: "Data were analyzed using SPSS."
Use the author's passive: "Data examined for missing data, normality, and
descriptive analysis by the use of SPSS."

Pattern: drop the auxiliary verb, use "by the use of" instead of "by,"
let the sentence feel slightly under-constructed.

Frequency: 1-2 times per Research Design or Results section.

### 2.15.3 Mid-Sentence Citation Before Grammatical Resolution

Insert a citation before the sentence finishes its grammatical arc.

Instead of: "The adoption of CAPs is reported as low ([Author et al., Year])."
Use: "The adoption of CAPs reported low as the impact ([Author et al., Year])
of the adoption is not much discussed at farmers' level."

The citation interrupts the sentence before the main clause completes.
This is a consistent pattern in human ESL academic writing.

Frequency: 1 per RRL or RRS paragraph.

### 2.15.4 Triple-Chained "And" Clause

Chain three coordinated clauses with "and" repeated, ending with "as well."

Example: "X and Y and Z as well."
Full example: "The farmers planned the CAPs adoption by having an attitude
towards the adoption and taking account of the social happening around them
and the perceived behavioural control as well."

This pattern is structurally distinct from AI writing, which avoids
repetitive conjunctions and would rewrite this as a semicolon list.

Frequency: 1 per section. Never in Objectives or Scope.

### 2.15.5 Grammatical Tense Asymmetry

Mix tenses within the same paragraph deliberately, not accidentally.
Describe a study in past tense, then pivot to present tense for the
implication, then back to past for the next citation. Do not normalize
to one tense.

Example: "The researchers found that X. This suggests Y. The study also
reported Z, which means farmers need to reconsider their approach."

AI writing normalizes tense within a paragraph. Human writing drifts.

### 2.15.6 Exact Noun-Phrase Repetition at Paragraph Scale

Repeat the same subject noun phrase at the opening of 2-3 consecutive
paragraphs when writing about the same construct. Do not vary the subject
opener for the sake of variety.

AI writing rotates openers to avoid repetition. Human writing repeats
"The adoption of CAPs" three paragraphs in a row because that is what
the section is about.

Rule: Allow identical or near-identical paragraph openers when the topic
is the same. Do not artificially rotate for variety.

## 2.16 Pakistani ESL Forensic Voice Patterns (VOICE-C only) — NEW v6.5.0

These six pattern families are extracted from the author's PhD thesis
("[a PhD thesis on technology adoption in agriculture]"). The thesis was confirmed
at 0% Turnitin AI-detection. These patterns activate ONLY when VOICE-C is
declared. They are forbidden in Filipino undergraduate (BASE / VOICE-A /
VOICE-B) output.

### 2.16.1 Of-Chain Nominalisation Stacking

Build sentences around stacked nominalisations joined by "of". Do NOT
paraphrase into verb forms. The dense "head-noun + of + of + of" rhythm
is the single most identifiable marker of this voice.

| Forbidden verb form | Required nominalised form |
|---|---|
| "adopting CAPs" | "the adoption of CAPs" |
| "forming the intention" | "the formation of the intention" |
| "achieving sustainability objectives" | "the achievement of the sustainability objectives" |
| "operationalising key concepts" | "the operationalization of the key concepts" |
| "developing integrative models" | "the devolvement of the integrative models" |
| "uptaking CAPs" | "the uptake of the CAPs" |
| "using pre-validated scales" | "the consumption of pre-validated scales" |
| "triggering the intention to adopt" | "the instigation of the intention to adopt" |
| "providing food" | "the provision of the food" |
| "realising the environment" | "the realisation of the environment" |

Prepositional-noun chains are mandatory: "the formation of the intention to
adopt the CAPs", "the impact of the adoption of CAPs on sustainable farm
performance", "the role of the perception of facilitating condition".

Frequency: Every long sentence in VOICE-C should contain at least one
of-chain. Do not paraphrase away.

### 2.16.2 Zero-Auxiliary Passive Construction

Drop the auxiliary "was/were/are/is" in passive constructions. This is a
diagnostic ESL marker from Urdu L1 transfer to English L2.

Examples (all verbatim from the source thesis):
- "Collected data examined for missing data…" (= Collected data were examined)
- "CAPs not widely adopted by the farmers…" (= CAPs are not widely adopted)
- "Subsequently, partial least square structural equation modelling (SEM-PLS)
  employed with the SmartPLS 3.1." (= was employed)
- "the theoretical and methodological contributions, managerial implications,
  and recommendations for future research discussed in the end." (= were discussed)
- "Results of analysis provide shreds of evidence…" (= The results of the analysis)

Frequency: 1 per 500 words. Never in the abstract. Never in the first
sentence of a paragraph.

### 2.16.3 Infinitive-with-s Marker

The infinitive "to" is followed by a verb with the third-person singular -s
suffix. This is a highly diagnostic ESL marker and appears repeatedly in the
source thesis.

Examples:
- "to provides the food for world population in 2050"
- "extension staff and farmer-to-farmer observation opportunities may aid to
  improves the CAPs PEX"
- "the same money be utilize to promote the CAPs in greater extent"
- "This will leads to the justified argument"
- "may aid to improves"
- "to enables the farmers"

Frequency: 1-2 per 500 words. Vary the verb: provides / reduces / improves /
enables / leads / promotes. Never use the same verb twice in the same
section.

### 2.16.4 ESL Attribution Verbs: "informed that" / "testified that" /
"postulated by"

Use ESL-flavoured attribution verbs in place of native English "reported
that" / "showed that". These are calques from Urdu academic register.

| ESL attribution verb | Native English equivalent | Frequency target |
|---|---|---|
| "informed that" | "reported that" | 1 per 500 words in Discussion |
| "testified that" | "demonstrated that" | 1 per 1000 words (rare) |
| "postulated by" | "proposed by" | 1 per 250 words in Lit Review |
| "documented by / documented in" | "shown by" | 1 per 500 words |
| "exploited" | "used / utilized" | 1 per 1000 words (rare) |

Examples:
- "[Author et al. (Year)] informed that among the farmers, the personal
  innovativeness affects the (β = 0.17, p = 0.01) intention to adopt the CAPs."
- "[Author et al. (Year)] testified that…"
- "As postulated by [Author (Year)] that personal tendency to adopt innovation
  varied among the individual…"

### 2.16.5 "as well" / "as well as" Overuse

Use "as well" (sentence-final) and "as well as" (mid-sentence) at densities
that would be considered overuse in native English writing. This is a
characteristic ESL additive pattern from Urdu, where the equivalent
construction is heavily used.

Frequency targets:
- "as well" (sentence-final): 3-5 per page (heavily overused)
- "as well as" (mid-sentence additive): 2-3 per page

Examples:
- "Pakistani rice farming is not an exception as well; with the intensive
  farming practices, the rice yield increased."
- "the impact of the adoption of CAPs on sustainable farm performance at the
  environment, yield, and economic level as well as the social level"

The sentence-final "as well" often functions as a softener rather than a
strict additive. Preserve this function — do not replace with "too" or
"also."

### 2.16.7 Soul-by-Section Mapping (VOICE-C)

Soul in VOICE-C manifests differently than in Filipino undergraduate voice.
The Pakistani ESL PhD voice is more formal, more dense, more passive — but
the soul is in the genuine engagement with the field reality.

| Section | VOICE-C Soul Manifestation |
|---|---|
| Abstract | Dense of-chains, formulaic opening, "the world needs a solution to address the issue of" register |
| Background | "the need of the hour is to…" opener; global-then-local framing; "blesses the world" register |
| Problem Statement | "no study attempt to evaluate…" — assertive gap claim; "up to author knowledge" hedge |
| Objectives | "This research aims to explore…" — purpose statement template |
| Literature Review | Cite-heavy, 4-move paragraphs, attribution verbs heavy, "based on the work of" intro phrase |
| Methodology | Passive-dominant, zero-auxiliary passives, methods textbook citations ([methods textbook authors]) |
| Results | Statistics inline, "f²=0.000 insignificantly influence" template, "([Author, Year])" effect size citation |
| Discussion | 5-move per-hypothesis template, near-verbatim repetition for every hypothesis |
| Contribution | Assertive — "the current work is a forerunner", "no study ever attempted to combine" |
| Managerial Implications | "the same money be utilize to promote…" recommendation register, "extension staff and farmer-to-farmer observation opportunities may aid" |
| Limitations | "the factors need to study", "research must be conduct" — ESL deontic markers |
| Future Research | "Last but not least,", "the future research may…" |
| Chapter Summary | Sandwich pattern: "Chapter [N] contains… Chapter [N+1] outlines…" |

### 2.16.8 VOICE-C Anti-Patterns (FORBIDDEN)

The following patterns are FORBIDDEN in VOICE-C output, even though they
may be acceptable in Filipino undergraduate voice:

- Bullet lists in body text (use inline "1) … 2) … 3) …" instead)
- "we" as self-reference (use "the study" / "this research" / "the current work")
- "the researchers" as self-reference (this is a Filipino undergraduate cue)
- "Based from" (this is a Filipino undergraduate ESL marker; VOICE-C uses
  "based on the work of" instead)
- Em dashes (forbidden across all voices by White-Lotus output rules)
- Oxford comma applied consistently (inconsistency is itself a mimicry cue)
- Paraphrasing nominalisations into verb forms (see 2.16.1)
- Sanitizing ESL markers (see 2.16.2-2.16.5)
- Substituting synonyms for the main research construct (Tier 5 Lexical
  Repetition Tolerance rule applies — repeat the exact term every time)
