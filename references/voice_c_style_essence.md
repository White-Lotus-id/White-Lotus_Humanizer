## Three Rules of Mimicry

### Rule of Mimicry #1 — The "Of-Chain" Sentence
Build sentences around stacked nominalisations joined by "of". Open with a
topic phrase, then pile on "of the [noun] of the [noun] of the [noun]". Drop
auxiliary "be" in passives. End with a parenthetical APA citation cluster.
Insert "as well" before the period.

**Pattern:**
> [Noun] of [noun] of [noun] [verb-past-participle] [preposition] [noun], as well as [noun] for the [noun] of the [noun] ([Author, Year]; [Author, Year]).

**Worked example:**
> "Achievement of sustainable agriculture is an enormously challenging task in the face of the environmental fluctuations experienced by the world at large."

### Rule of Mimicry #2 — The Connector Regime
Begin nearly every paragraph — and most sentences inside paragraphs — with one
of: *Moreover, / However, / Furthermore, / Nevertheless, / Additionally,
/ Therefore, / On the other hand, / Likewise, / Similarly,*. Use "as well" or
"as well as" to add a second clause rather than a coordinating conjunction.
Use "However," to introduce the inevitable qualification that follows any
positive claim. Use "Nevertheless," only when the qualification is concessive.

### Rule of Mimicry #3 — The ESL Diagnostic Bundle
A faithful output MUST reproduce — at a moderate density of 1-2 per paragraph —
the following ESL markers:
- Dropped definite article: "Adoption of innovation is complex…"
- Infinitive with -s: "to provides / to reduces / to improves"
- Zero-auxiliary passive: "Collected data examined…"
- "informed that" / "testified that" / "postulated by" as attribution verbs
- "need to" as deontic instead of "must / should"
- "based on the work of" to introduce any borrowed concept
- "up to author knowledge" / "to the best of author knowledge"
- British spelling throughout (fertiliser, utilised, behaviour, modelling,
  operationalisation)
- Single quotes for coined terms: 'black-box', 'what works'
- Inline numbered lists as "1) … 2) … 3) …"
- "i.e., " and "e.g., " mid-sentence to introduce lists/parentheticals

## Connector Inventory (rotate, do not repeat consecutively)

1. **Moreover,** — most overused; can open two consecutive paragraphs but not
   two consecutive sentences.
2. **However,** — almost always sentence-initial, never mid-sentence.
3. **Furthermore,** — additive, sentence-initial.
4. **Nevertheless,** — concessive, sentence-initial; heavy in Discussion for
   contrasting findings.
5. **Additionally,** — additive, sentence-initial.
6. **Therefore,** — causal, sentence-initial.
7. **On the other hand,** — contrastive.
8. **Likewise, / Similarly,** — comparative.
9. **Henceforth,** — consequence; ESL-flavoured (used in place of "Hence /
   Therefore").
10. **In conclusion,** — section-closer (Discussion & Contribution sections).
11. **Contrarily, / Conversely,** — contrastive.
12. **Consequently,** — consequence.
13. **Based on the discussion mentioned above,** — section bridge.
14. **In line with this study research objective,** — bridge to model/framework.
15. **Last but not least,** — list-closer.

## Per-Hypothesis Discussion Template (repeats near-verbatim)

```
The study's next hypotheses bid to evaluate the impact of [CONSTRUCT GROUP]
on the intention to adopt the [TOPIC]. Study the [Nth] hypothesis explores
the impact of the a) [CONSTRUCT-1], b) [CONSTRUCT-2], and c) [CONSTRUCT-3]
on the intention to adopt [TOPIC].

The study result shows that the [CONSTRUCT] ([f²=X.XXX]) [significantly/
insignificantly] influence the intention to adopt [TOPIC] ([Author, Year]),
and [offer / provide] [no] support to accept the [Hxx].

The [insignificant / significant] impact of [CONSTRUCT] on the intention
to adopt [TOPIC] also documented by [Author et al., Year], and [Author
et al., Year].

The result of the study highlights [the lack of awareness among the
farmers / the reportedly low perception of TOPIC performance / ...].

However, it is essential to note that [CONSTRUCT] for the [TOPIC] needs
[improvement / promotion]; [extension staff / policymaker / researcher]
and [stakeholder] opportunities may aid to improves the [TOPIC]
[CONSTRUCT] and adoption of [TOPIC] as well ([Author, Year]).
```

This 5-move template is repeated almost verbatim for every direct effect and
every moderation hypothesis.

### Hypothesis-result template (Discussion)
> "The study result shows that the [CONSTRUCT] ([f²=…]) [significantly/insignificantly] influence the intention to adopt CAPs ([Author, Year]), and [offer / provide] [no] support to accept the [Hxx]."

### Hypothesis-result-lit-comparison template
> "The [insignificant / significant] [impact / effect] of [CONSTRUCT] on the intention to adopt CAPs [matches / documented by / agrees with] the results reported by the [Author et al., Year]."

### Hypothesis-recommendation template
> "However, it is essential to note that [CONSTRUCT] for the CAPs needs [improvement / promotion]; extension staff and farmer-to-farmer observation opportunities may aid to improves the CAPs [CONSTRUCT] and adoption of CAPs as well ([Author, Year])."

### Citation-introduction template
> "For this work, [CONCEPT] [conceptualisation / consumption / borrowed] from the work of [Author (Year)]."

### Hypothesis-lead template (Lit Review)
> "This discussion pinpoint to hypotheses for the study as that:"

### Method-purpose template
> "[Method] utilised to [estimate / evaluate / analyse] [construct] for the study."

### Conclusion template
> "Addressing the issues of [topic], this work [analysed / explored] [construct] based on [factors]. The results of data analysis offer different conclusions."

## Fake Abstract Example (Style Demonstration)

> The digital revolution blesses the world with the development and richness of human life on earth. However, the digital revolution caused an upsurge in global healthcare expenditure and the rise of patients' waiting time on earth. The world needs a solution to address the issue of medical errors (MEs), as world healthcare is the 3rd most significant contributor to MEs. The solution for reducing MEs from the healthcare sector is to use artificial intelligence practices (AIPs). However, AIPs not widely adopted by the clinicians of developing countries. Adoption of innovation is complex, as numerous factors impact the clinicians at various levels enabling the adoption of AIPs. Moreover, the adoption of AIPs reported low as the impact of the adoption is not much discussed at clinicians' and hospital level. This research aims to explore the AIPs adoption as a process based on the mix of the clinician personal inclination towards the patient safety, trust on hospital management, innovativeness, accuracy orientation, and risk-taking attitude as well as attributes of the artificial intelligence practices like performance expectancy, effort expectancy, and social influence for the formation of the intention to adopt the AIPs in Pakistani clinicians.

### Mimicry cues present in the fake abstract
- Formulaic "blesses the world" opening
- "3rd most significant contributor" formulation
- Zero-auxiliary passive: "AIPs not widely adopted"
- Dropped article: "Adoption of innovation is complex"
- "Moreover, … reported low" connector
- "as well as" mid-sentence additive
- Nominalisation chain: "the formation of the intention to adopt the AIPs"
- British spelling throughout
- "for the formation of" prepositional close
- "in Pakistani clinicians" ESL closing

## Quick-Reference Cheat Sheet

| Dimension | Rule |
|---|---|
| Register | Formal academic, ESL flavour, Commonwealth spelling |
| Acronyms | Define once in parentheses, then use exclusively; maintain a List of Abbreviation |
| Sentences | Long (35-60 words), compound-complex, passive-dominant |
| Openers | Subject+verb OR transition+comma; rarely anything else |
| Transitions | Moreover, / However, / Furthermore, / Nevertheless, / Additionally, / Therefore, / On the other hand, |
| Additives | "as well" (sentence-final), "as well as" (mid-sentence) — heavily overused |
| Citations | APA, parenthetical end-of-sentence clusters of 2-3 refs |
| Attribution verbs | reported / postulated / documented / informed that / testified that |
| Hedging | reportedly, likely, possibly, may, can, need to, it is essential to note that |
| Paragraphs | 4-8 sentences, ~150-250 words, 4-move structure (Topic→Evidence→Citation→Bridge) |
| Articles | Frequently dropped ("the" and "a") |
| Auxiliaries | Frequently dropped in passives ("Collected data examined…") |
| Infinitives | Often take -s ("to provides / to reduces / to improves") |
| Modals | "be utilize", "be conduct" — be + past participle without modal |
| Lists | Inline "1) … 2) … 3) …" — almost never bulleted |
| Quotes | Single quotes for coined terms ('black-box', 'what works') |
| Statistics inline | "(β = 0.017, p = 0.01)" — embedded mid-sentence without commas |
| Chapter frame | Every chapter opens with "This chapter…" preview and closes with "Chapter Summary" + "Chapter [N+1]…" lookahead |
| Per-hypothesis template (Discussion) | Topic → Result with stats → Lit comparison → Interpretation → "However, it is essential to note that…" recommendation |
