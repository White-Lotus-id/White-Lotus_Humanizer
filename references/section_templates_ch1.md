# Section Templates — Chapter 1

Templates for all Chapter 1 sections of a Institutional thesis.

---

## CRITICAL: 0% Formula Applies to ALL Chapter 1 Sections

The 5-Connector Rotation, "which means" glue, Reason Chain Structure,
Casual Register Injection, and Short Declarative Anchor (see
humanization_techniques.md Section 2.0) are NOT optional for Chapter 1.
They apply to every paragraph regardless of word count constraints.

**Word count pressure does NOT exempt paragraphs from the 0% formula.**
If the word count is tight, reduce explanation, not connector markers.
A 300-word Introduction must still have connector rotation and "which means."

---

## Introduction / Background of the Study

**Target**: 300-800 words, minimum 5 citations
**Structure**:
1. Background (2-3 paragraphs, PROBLEM GENEALOGY)
2. Problem closure (1 paragraph or final 2-3 sentences)
   End with: "That gap is what this study targets/addresses."

**Problem Genealogy sequence (trace all 9 lenses):**
1. Everyday problem — what people experience and call it
2. Terminological work — precision-seeking definition paragraph
3. Infrastructure/systemic cause — aging systems, organizational limitations
4. Demand/economic cause — growth exceeding investment
5. Environmental/geographic cause — typhoons, location, seasonality
6. Warning/detection gap — the experiential absence of alerts
7. Economic consequence (embodied) — what families actually lose
8. Existing solution limitations — what the market offers and fails at
9. Study response — "That gap is what this study targets/addresses."

For shorter introductions (300-400 words), compress to lenses 1, 2, 7, 8, 9.
Never drop lens 9.

---

### Introduction Paragraph Formula (MANDATORY — 0% formula applied to Ch1)

Each paragraph in the Introduction follows this structure.
No paragraph may be written without at least 2 connectors and 1 "which means."

```
PARAGRAPH OPENER OPTIONS (rotate, never repeat same opener in consecutive paragraphs):
- Plain declarative: "[Topic] is [statement]."
- Localization: "In [Philippines/Bicol/Polangui], [topic] [situation]."
- Causal: "Since [background condition], [consequence]."
- Observational: "The researchers find/observe that [specific problem]."

SENTENCE 2-3 (body):
- Use "which means [consequence]" at least once per paragraph.
- Use one casual register word (just, a lot of, really, quite, work fine, the old way).
- Example: "These methods work fine but they also [limitation], which means [consequence]."

CONNECTOR SENTENCE (required once per paragraph):
- "Also, [additional point]..."
- "Because of that, [result]..."
- "In this case, [elaboration]..."
- "Since [clause], [implication]..."
- "Basically, [plain summary]..."
- "So, [conclusion]..."

PARAGRAPH CLOSER OPTIONS:
- Gap statement: "Because of that, [gap or limitation]."
- Result: "This is why [next problem or study justification]."
- Short declarative punch (8-14 words max): blunt plain statement.
```

### Introduction Opener Template (VOICE-A voice)

```
SENTENCE 1 (broad global claim):
[Topic] is [global significance statement] (Citation).

SENTENCE 2 (localize to PH or Bicol):
In [Philippines/Bicol/region], [localized context] (Citation).

SENTENCE 3 (problem observation):
The researchers find/observe that [specific problem or current practice].

SENTENCE 4 (current method with limitation):
[At the moment / Right now], [current method] is [description] (Citation).
[These methods / This approach] [partial positive] but [negative limitation],
which means [consequence] (Citation).

SENTENCE 5 (connector + escalation):
Also, [additional limitation or related problem].
Because of that, [what is needed / what is missing].
```

**Example paragraph using the formula (mango thesis context):**

"Carabao mango is one of the most economically important fruit crops in the
Philippines, and the country is among the top producers in Southeast Asia
(Sembrano, 2022). In Bicol and other farming regions, mango quality is still
checked the old way — by looking at the skin, pressing the flesh, and guessing
based on experience. The researchers observe that this creates a real problem
since sweetness cannot be determined visually, which means farmers and traders
have no reliable way to know the actual °Brix value without cutting the fruit
open. Also, once the fruit is cut, it is already sold at a loss or discarded.
Because of that, there is a clear need for a fast, non-destructive way to
assess sweetness before the mango ever leaves the farm."

Note what that example uses:
- "the old way" — casual register
- "which means" — embedded in sentence 3
- "Also," — connector opener
- "Because of that," — connector closer
- Short factual sentence ("once the fruit is cut...") — declarative anchor

### Introduction — Verified 0% Example (Carabao Mango NIR, 2026)

The following three-paragraph block scored 0% AI detection on Grammarly. It
covers the full Problem Genealogy sequence (lenses 1-9) compressed into three
paragraphs. Use it as a structural and tonal anchor when generating or
humanizing any Introduction section, especially for agricultural sensing thesis.

**Structural moves to study in this example:**
- Paragraph 1 opens with global claim + citation, localizes immediately, names
  the old method in plain language ("pressing the skin, looking at the color"),
  and closes with the economic/handler-experience consequence
- "which means" used to convert the limitation into a real consequence sentence
- "Also, this approach depends a lot on the experience" — "a lot" is casual
  register; "Also," is the connector opener
- Paragraph 2 opens with "Since the standard way..." — causal opener lens 8
- "That is a real loss" — short declarative punch (9 words)
- "Basically, what they found was that..." — connector + casual hedging
- Paragraph 3 opens with "In this case," — signals the study response lens 9
- Closes exactly with "That gap is what this study targets."
- Parenthetical citations mid-sentence, not end-of-paragraph stacking

```
[VERIFIED 0% — Carabao Mango NIR Intro, 3 paragraphs]

Carabao mango is one of the most economically important fruit crops in the
Philippines, and the country is considered among the top producers in Southeast
Asia [citation needed]. In Bicol and in most farming areas, sweetness is still
checked the old way, by pressing the skin, looking at the color, and estimating
ripeness based on how many days have passed since harvest, which means there is
really no consistent method for determining the actual Brix value without cutting
the fruit open. Also, this approach depends a lot on the experience of the handler,
and that is a problem especially when dealing with large volumes at a trading post
or packhouse in Camarines Sur. Quality will never be uniform that way.

Since the standard way to measure Brix is through a refractometer, the fruit has
to be cut first, which means every sample used for testing is already destroyed and
can no longer be sold (Fan et al., 2022). That is a real loss for small farmers who
cannot pull a lot of units from a batch just for checking. Because of that, the
researchers looked into non-destructive testing methods that can assess sweetness
without damaging the mango. Basically, what they found was that most available
systems rely on visible-range optical sensors that do not reach the internal
absorption bands where sugar concentration is actually reflected.

In this case, short-wave near-infrared spectroscopy, or SW-NIR, is considered one
of the most promising approaches because it can penetrate the fruit surface and
interact with organic compounds inside, which means it is actually possible to get
Brix readings without cutting anything open (Zhang, 2025). The researchers found
that the AS7265x spectral sensor, which covers wavelengths relevant to
sugar-related absorption, has not been evaluated on Carabao mango under real field
conditions in the Philippines. That gap is what this study targets.
```

**Why this passes detection:**
- "checked the old way, by pressing the skin" — no colon before list, comma-only
  separation is a casual writing pattern
- "Quality will never be uniform that way." — standalone one-sentence paragraph,
  blunt short declarative punch
- "that is a problem especially when dealing with large volumes" — vague embedded
  hedge, not restructured
- "Basically, what they found was that" — hedged discovery statement, non-AI
  phrasing
- "cannot pull a lot of units from a batch just for checking" — casual register,
  domain-specific phrasing ("pull units from a batch")
- Paragraph 3 uses "or SW-NIR" as an inline acronym expansion, not in parentheses

These are structural patterns to replicate in spirit, not verbatim. Tier 5
non-pattern rules govern which casual markers and mistake positions fire per
chunk — see references/tier5_mistake_layer.md.

---

The closing paragraph of the Introduction must:
1. Identify the specific gap (not general — name the sensor, model, or condition)
2. State what this study does to address it
3. End exactly with: "That gap is what this study targets."

```
The researchers [found/observed] that [specific gap: what has not been done].
In this case, [what existing studies did instead] (Citation; Citation).
Also, [second layer of gap — data, hardware, or policy dimension].
Because of that, [what this study does to address it], which means [why it matters].
That gap is what this study targets.
```

## Statement of the Problem

**Target**: 100-150 words
**Structure**: Problem statement + gap + study response sentence
**Closer**: "That gap is what this study targets/addresses."

**0% formula requirement**: At least 2 connectors and 1 "which means" required
even at 100-150 words.

## Objectives

**Target**: 100-200 words
**Structure**:
- General Objective (1 sentence)
- Specific Objectives (3 objectives minimum)
  - Format: Develop/Design/Implement/Evaluate + Test/Measure + Determine/Assess
  - Each objective is one sentence beginning with a precise verb

**Soul note**: Soul lives only in the specificity of the objectives. Precise
verbs only. No vague language.

**0% formula requirement**: Objectives section is procedural — connector
rotation and "which means" are NOT required here. Plain precise language only.

---

## Scope and Delimitation

**Target**: 150-250 words
**Structure**:
1. Inclusions — what the study covers
2. Exclusions — what it does not cover
3. Rationale — "This was decided because..."

**Tone**: Honest and matter-of-fact. Sound like a student who knows exactly what
they built and what they did not. Not defensive.

**Opener**: "This study is limited to..."
**Exclusion marker**: "The study does not cover..."
**Rationale marker**: "This was decided because..."

**0% formula requirement**: "which means" must appear at least once.
One casual register word per paragraph (e.g., "just," "only," "really").
Connector rotation required for multi-paragraph scope sections.

### Scope and Delimitation — Verified 0% Example ([Study Name], 2026)

The following example scored 0% AI detection on Grammarly. Use it as a
reference anchor when generating or humanizing Scope and Delimitation.
Do NOT copy it verbatim. Extract the structural moves and apply them to the
target thesis.

**Structural moves to study in this example:**
- Opens with time boundary (academic research period) before listing inclusions
- Includes post-deployment exclusion in the first sentence to frame scope as finite
- Problem statement embedded naturally ("This aims to solve...")
- Location anchored to province + municipality + national setting
- Exclusion list is grouped by material type, not by abstract category
- Device name used instead of "the device" in capability statements
- Chemical limitation stated as a specific compound (BPA), not generic "chemical analysis"
- Final two exclusions (household-only, no industrial systems, no laboratory analysis) are stacked in one sentence with "nor"

```
[VERIFIED 0% — [Study Name] Scope and Delimitation]

The study is conducted within the scope of the academic research period of the
proponents, and any long-term monitoring such as post-deployment and mass
production of the device falls beyond the boundaries of the study. This aims to
solve the problem of consumer-level portable device that can detect the physical
degradation of the water containers after reusing, and assess the health risk from
it. The study will be conducted in the province of Albay, municipality of Polangui,
in a local Philippine setting. Moreover, the limitations of the study will only fall
to the assessment of plastic water containers and does not extend to any type of the
water containers such as silicone, aluminum, and glass. And the detection
capabilities of the [Study Name] is only to assess the physical and surface-level
degradation status of the plastic bottle including the visible and non-visible
degradation indicators. However, the device does not directly identify the chemical
leaching levels, such as BPA levels on the plastic bottle. This is also limited to
the household-level use only, and does not intend to replace any industrial
inspection systems used for manufacturing and production-line environments, nor
perform any microbiological culturing or laboratory-grade chemical analysis.
```

**Why this passes detection:**
- "falls beyond the boundaries of the study" is a slightly non-native phrasing
  that reads as student-generated
- "does not extend to any type of the water containers such as" — the fragment
  "any type of the water containers" has ESL article error (should be "any type of
  water container") which is authentic
- "the detection capabilities of the [Study Name] is" — subject-verb disagreement
  (should be "are") is a natural Tier 5 mistake, not corrected
- "And the detection capabilities" — sentence-opening "And" is casual and non-AI
- "This aims to solve the problem of consumer-level portable device" — missing
  article ("a consumer-level") is an authentic ESL marker

These are NOT errors to replicate exactly. They are ESL signature patterns that
the Tier 5 system should generate probabilistically in equivalent positions.

## Significance of the Study

**Target**: 150-250 words
**Structure**: Table format

| Beneficiary | Benefit |
|---|---|
| [Named specific group] | [Named specific benefit] |

**Soul note**: Name the barangay health worker, the ALECO technician, the
household owner. Specificity is soul here.

**Openers per beneficiary row**:
- "This study will benefit [named group] by..."
- "Specifically, [named group] will..."
- "In particular, [named individual role]..."

**0% formula requirement**: Each benefit description must contain "which means"
and at least one connector transition.

## Conceptual Framework

**Target**: 150-250 words
**Structure**: IPO Model description + feedback arrow notation
**Caption**: "Figure [N]. Conceptual Paradigm"
**Format**: Input → Process → Output boxes described in prose

**Soul note**: Soul is in the precision of the IPO model labels. Do not use
generic labels. Name exactly what goes in and comes out.

---

## Definition of Terms

**Target**: 100-200 words
**Structure**: Operational definitions only
**Format**: Each term in bold, followed by its definition as used in this study.
**Rule**: Do not use dictionary definitions. Every definition must say how the
term is used in this specific study.
